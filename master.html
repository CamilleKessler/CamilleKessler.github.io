Master's thesis

Alpine ibex sequencing assay and immunogenetics

3 October 2023

Alpine ibex is an emblematic alpine species that was severely impacted by overhunting in the 1800's to the point that there were only about 100 individuals left. Ironically, the species was saved because then King of Italy, Vittorio Emanuele II, loved ibex hunting so much that he protected the species and provided a refuge in what is now the Gran Paradiso National Park. All existing populations, with a total of about 50,000 individuals, stem from this single refugial population. The bottleneck left distinct genomic traces, including one of particular concern: low genetic diversity at the MHC. The MHC is a region of the genome that codes for pathogen recognition proteins, the more diverse this region is, the more resistant an individual is to diseases. Low diversity at this region can therefore lad to higher disease susceptibility. Interestingly, in alpine ibex, some variation at the MHC was produced through introgression from domestic goat.

This is where my Master's thesis comes into play. I carried it out at the Université de Neuchâtel in Switzerland under the supervision of Prof. Daniel Croll and in collaboration with Prof. Christine Grossen of the University of Zürich. The aim was to produce a sequencing assay to monitor the alpine ibex populations. For this, we designed and validated a microfluidic assay of about 1,000 amplicons that can be used for easy and cost-effective population monitoring of alpine ibex. The assay simultaneously screens for population structure, immunogenetics and hybridisation with domestic goat, and perfoms well in comparison with RAD-seq and low coverage WGS data. With our assay, we were able to reconstruct the reintroduction history of alpine ibex with the Gran Paradiso population as the source of all other and each reintroduction represented a bottleneck event with a loss of genetic diversity. We were also able to confirm the identity of seven suspected hybrids, of which only three were actual hybrids. Finally, the analysis of immunity-related loci confirmed the low genetic diversity at the MHC previously observed in this species.
